# AER Behavioral Measurement Model — Comparison with Industry Standards

**Prosci ADKAR | Kotter 8-Step | McKinsey 7-S | Employee Engagement Surveys | Net Promoter Score**

A comparison of the Insheights AER Behavioral Measurement Model against established change management, organizational health, and customer measurement frameworks.

---

## Positioning

AER (Adoption | Engagement | Retention) measures behavioral response to value delivery across two parallel tracks — Customer AER and Organizational AER. It occupies the behavioral signal layer between 3E (how value is delivered) and the 8 Values (what the organization generates as a result).

The frameworks below each capture a portion of what AER measures. None covers both tracks simultaneously, and none embeds behavioral measurement within a connected value delivery architecture.

## Comparison by Framework

### Prosci ADKAR

ADKAR (Awareness → Desire → Knowledge → Ability → Reinforcement) is the leading individual-level change management model. It diagnoses where a specific person is blocked in their change journey.

**Alignment:** ADKAR's Ability stage maps to AER Adoption — the person can do it and is doing it. Reinforcement maps partially to AER Retention — the behavior is sustained. Desire sits closest to AER Engagement, though it is attitudinal rather than behavioral.

**Key difference:** ADKAR is a blockage diagnostic for individuals; AER is a behavioral signal model for systems. ADKAR tells a change manager where to intervene with a specific person. AER tells a leader whether the operating model is producing behavioral change across the organization. They are complementary: ADKAR informs individual coaching; AER measures aggregate outcome.

**AER gap filled:** ADKAR has no customer dimension and does not connect individual change behavior to organizational value generation.

---

### Kotter's 8-Step Model

Kotter sequences the leadership actions required to execute large-scale change: establish urgency, build a guiding coalition, form a strategic vision, enlist volunteers, enable action, generate short-term wins, sustain acceleration, and institute change.

**Alignment:** Kotter's later steps — generating wins, consolidating gains, anchoring new approaches — correspond to what AER Retention measures: did the change become the way we work?

**Key difference:** Kotter prescribes what leaders should do; AER measures whether what they did worked. Kotter's model is action-oriented and largely silent on measurement. AER fills Kotter's largest gap by providing behavioral signals that confirm whether Steps 5–8 produced lasting change or surface-level compliance.

**AER gap filled:** No measurement construct. No customer dimension. No connection to value outcomes.

---

### McKinsey 7-S

7-S maps whether seven organizational elements — Strategy, Structure, Systems, Staff, Skills, Style, and Shared Values — are mutually aligned. It is a systemic diagnostic used to identify misalignment that blocks execution.

**Alignment:** 7-S describes organizational conditions; AER measures behavioral response to those conditions. A 7-S misalignment in Systems or Style would surface as an Adoption or Engagement gap in Org AER.

**Key difference:** 7-S is structural and diagnostic; AER is behavioral and signal-based. 7-S explains why adoption might be failing; AER is what tells you it is failing. The two are complementary as a diagnostic stack: AER surfaces the behavioral signal, 7-S helps identify the structural cause.

**AER gap filled:** No behavioral measurement. No customer dimension. No leading indicator function.

---

### Employee Engagement Surveys (Gallup Q12 and equivalents)

Employee engagement surveys measure how employees feel about their work, manager, team, and organization. The Gallup Q12 and similar instruments capture attitudinal indicators — belonging, purpose, recognition, and development.

**Alignment:** Org AER Engagement is the behavioral counterpart to the attitudinal engagement survey. Both target organizational health and the quality of employee participation. AER explicitly acknowledges this parallel: like engagement surveys, it captures organizational response — but through behavior rather than sentiment.

**Key difference:** Engagement surveys measure how people feel; AER measures what people do. A team can score well on a satisfaction survey and still fail to adopt new practices or leave at elevated rates. Behavioral signals are more directly predictive of operational and change outcomes than attitudinal ones.

**AER gap filled:** Attitudinal only — no behavioral dimension. No customer track. No connection to value delivery architecture or leading indicator sequencing.

---

### Net Promoter Score

NPS measures customer likelihood to recommend, used as a proxy for customer loyalty and satisfaction. It is the most widely deployed single-metric customer measurement instrument.

**Alignment:** Customer AER is the behavioral counterpart to NPS. AER's Retention/Recommendation variant captures the same advocacy outcome NPS targets — but as observed behavior (customers who stayed, returned, and referred) rather than stated likelihood. NPS asks "would you?"; AER measures "did you?"

**Key difference:** NPS is a stated-likelihood metric derived from a survey; AER is a behavioral signal derived from observed customer action. AER treats Recommendation as a form of Retention — the sustained behavioral proof that value was realized, expressed through advocacy. A customer can give a high NPS score and still churn; AER captures the behavioral reality.

**AER gap filled:** Attitudinal / stated likelihood only. No organizational track. No connection to delivery system or value architecture.

## The Distinctive Contribution of AER

### Dual-Track Structure

No industry standard framework measures customer behavioral response and organizational behavioral response within a single model. AER runs both tracks in parallel because organizational value depends on both simultaneously. A delivery system that generates customer adoption while losing organizational capability is not sustainable. A delivery system with strong internal engagement that fails to produce customer retention is not creating value. The dual track is the structural insight that no established framework captures.

### Leading Indicator Architecture

AER occupies the middle layer of a three-layer measurement sequence: 3E signals surface first (operational), AER signals surface next (behavioral), 8 Values signals surface last (organizational impact). This sequencing makes AER a leading indicator of value performance — a decline in Adoption or Engagement is visible before it registers as Customer, Financial, or People Value degradation. Established frameworks either measure lagging outcomes (NPS, financial metrics) or diagnose structural conditions (7-S, ADKAR). AER is positioned to act as an early warning system within a connected architecture.

### Behavioral, Not Attitudinal

AER measures what customers and teams do, not how they feel. Satisfaction is a condition; behavior is the signal. This distinction — shared with NPS and engagement surveys at the instrument level, but applied more consistently — means AER metrics are directly actionable: adoption rates, engagement depth, retention curves, and recommendation frequency are observable, trackable, and predictive in ways that sentiment scores alone are not.
