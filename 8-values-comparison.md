# Insheights 8 Organizational Values Measurement Model — Comparison with Industry Standards

**Balanced Scorecard | IIRC 6 Capitals | EFQM Excellence Model | ESG / GRI Standards | OKRs**

A comparison of the Insheights 8 Organizational Values Measurement Model against established strategic performance, organizational excellence, integrated reporting, and goal-setting frameworks.

---

## Positioning

The Insheights 8 Values model measures organizational value generation — what the organization produces across eight interconnected dimensions as a result of its strategy, transformation, and delivery operating through the IIVCS. It occupies the organizational outcome layer of the measurement architecture: downstream of 3E (how value is delivered) and AER (behavioral response to delivery), and upstream of governance and external reporting.

Each of the frameworks below captures part of what the 8 Values measures. What distinguishes the 8 Values among them is the combination of three choices: tracing value generation to an operational delivery system (OSM/PTS/CVDM), modeling compounding relationships between dimensions rather than treating them independently, and positioning value measurement as a leading-indicator layer between delivery signals and governance reporting. The Balanced Scorecard's strategy maps are the closest precedent for the compounding idea (see below).

## Comparison by Framework

### Balanced Scorecard (Kaplan & Norton)

The Balanced Scorecard measures organizational performance across four perspectives: Financial, Customer, Internal Processes, and Learning & Growth. It is the most widely deployed strategic measurement framework globally, used to translate strategy into operational metrics and track execution across dimensions that financial reporting alone cannot capture.

**Alignment:** The BSC's four perspectives map directly to 8 Values dimensions — Financial (Financial Value), Customer (Customer Value + Market Value), Internal Processes (Operational Value), Learning & Growth (People Value + Innovation Value). The BSC introduced the principle that organizational performance is multi-dimensional, which is the same foundational claim the 8 Values makes. Strategy maps — the BSC's mechanism for showing cause-and-effect between dimensions — are the closest analog to the 8 Values compounding model.

**Key difference:** The BSC measures performance *toward* a strategy; the 8 Values measures value *generated through* an operating system. The BSC's four perspectives are treated as independent measurement buckets — a customer metric does not feed a financial metric in the BSC model except through strategy map inference. The 8 Values explicitly models the compounding relationships: Customer Value behavioral signals drive Financial Value; People Value health drives Operational Value capability stock; Market Value is earned through Customer Value and Strategic Value consistency, not set independently. The BSC asks "are we meeting targets in each dimension?" The 8 Values asks "which dimensions are generating, which are consuming, and where is the upstream cause?"

The BSC does pair leading and lagging indicators — performance drivers ahead of outcomes — but that lead/lag logic is constructed per strategy map and left to the practitioner to define, and in practice most BSC implementations collapse to outcome measurement. The 8 Values sits within a three-layer sequence — 3E (leading), AER (bridge), 8 Values (organizational outcome) — that makes leading indicators structurally explicit and connected to delivery, not inferred per implementation.

**BSC gap filled by 8 Values:** No delivery architecture connection. No compounding model between perspectives. No behavioral signal layer. No bridge to integrated reporting standards.

### IIRC 6 Capitals (International Integrated Reporting Council)

The IIRC 6 Capitals framework defines six forms of capital that organizations depend on and affect: Financial, Manufactured, Intellectual, Human, Social & Relationship, and Natural. It is the foundation of Integrated Reporting — the most significant evolution in corporate reporting since the introduction of the income statement — and is used by boards and investors to understand how organizations create, preserve, and erode value across all capital forms.

**Alignment:** The 8 Values model is designed to bridge directly to IR 6 Capitals. The 8 Values measure the *flow* of value generation; IR 6 Capitals measures the *stock* of capital held. Every value dimension maps to one or more capitals: Customer Value and Market Value → Social & Relationship Capital; Financial Value → Financial Capital; People Value → Human Capital; Operational Value → Manufactured and Intellectual Capital; Strategic Value and Innovation Value → Intellectual Capital; Impact Value → Natural and Social Capital. The bridge is documented within the 8 Values model, enabling organizations using the IIVCS to report in IR-compatible terms without building a separate measurement system.

**Key difference:** IR 6 Capitals is a *reporting* framework for external stakeholders. It tells investors and boards what capital the organization holds — the stock at a point in time. It does not tell the operating team how that capital is being generated, through which mechanisms, or where generation is failing. An IR report confirms that Human Capital increased; it does not explain whether that increase came from People Value improvements in the operating model, from an acquisition, or from a favourable market for talent. The 8 Values is an *operational measurement* framework — it traces capital generation to its source in the IIVCS delivery architecture. The IR 6 Capitals report is the external destination for what the 8 Values produces internally.

**IR 6 Capitals gap filled by 8 Values:** No operational grounding — does not explain how capitals are generated. No delivery connection. No leading indicator layer. No compounding model between capital forms. No mechanism for internal governance; designed exclusively for external reporting.

### EFQM Excellence Model

The EFQM Excellence Model is the leading organizational quality framework in Europe, used by organizations across sectors to assess and improve organizational performance. It evaluates organizations across two domains: Enablers (Leadership, Strategy, People, Partnerships & Resources, Processes, Products & Services) and Results (Customer Results, People Results, Society Results, Business Results). Organizations are scored using the RADAR methodology — Results, Approaches, Deployment, Assessment and Refinement.

**Alignment:** EFQM covers the broadest range of dimensions of any framework in this comparison. Leadership maps to People Value organizational dimension. Strategy maps to Strategic Value. People maps to People Value. Processes maps to Operational Value. Customer Results maps to Customer Value. People Results maps to People Value. Society Results maps to Impact Value. Business Results maps to Financial Value and Market Value. EFQM's separation of Enablers from Results recognizes that organizational conditions (Enablers) determine organizational outputs (Results) — the same directional logic that drives the 8 Values compounding model.

**Key difference:** EFQM is a quality *assessment* framework — organizations are evaluated by trained assessors against defined criteria and scored against a benchmark. The 8 Values is a continuous *operational measurement* model embedded in a delivery architecture. EFQM tells you where you are relative to an excellence standard at a point in time; the 8 Values tells you how value is being generated and where it is compounding or leaking on an ongoing basis. EFQM produces a score; the 8 Values produces a signal.

EFQM's Enablers and Results are analytically separated — the model acknowledges that Enablers influence Results but does not operationalize the compounding relationships between specific result dimensions. Customer Results and People Results in EFQM are independent scoring categories; in the 8 Values, Customer Value and People Value are explicitly connected (People Value → Operational Value → Customer Value delivery chain). EFQM diagnoses organizational conditions; AER measures what those conditions produce behaviorally; the 8 Values measures what those behaviors generate as organizational value.

**EFQM gap filled by 8 Values:** No delivery connection — EFQM diagnoses conditions, not mechanisms. No behavioral signal layer. No compounding model between result dimensions. No leading indicator architecture. EFQM is periodic and assessor-driven; the 8 Values is continuous and internally operated.

### ESG / GRI Standards (Environmental, Social, Governance)

ESG frameworks — including the Global Reporting Initiative (GRI), SASB (Sustainability Accounting Standards Board), TCFD (Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures), and their convergence in the ISSB (International Sustainability Standards Board) — define how organizations measure and disclose their environmental, social, and governance performance. ESG is the dominant external accountability and disclosure framework for non-financial organizational impact.

**Alignment:** Impact Value is the dimension of the 8 Values most directly addressed by ESG frameworks. The external layer of Impact Value — societal, environmental, and community contribution — maps to the Environmental and Social dimensions of ESG. The internal layer — mission advancement and governance integrity — maps to the Governance dimension. The bridge between Impact Value and Natural and Social Capital in the IR 6 Capitals framework connects to TCFD (Natural) and GRI (Social) reporting respectively. Organizations using the 8 Values to measure Impact Value generation are producing the operational data that feeds ESG disclosures.

**Key difference:** ESG frameworks are disclosure and accountability instruments — they define what organizations must report to external stakeholders, regulators, and rating agencies. They are not operational measurement frameworks. ESG tells the market how the organization performed against sustainability metrics; it does not tell the organization how its operational behavior is generating or eroding impact. Impact Value treats mission and societal contribution as an *operational dimension* that compounds internally — strong Impact Value attracts People Value (people want to work for organizations with genuine purpose), amplifies Market Value (stakeholders trust organizations that live their stated values), and deepens Customer Value (customers trust organizations with integrity). ESG frameworks capture the output of that compounding; the 8 Values models the mechanism.

ESG frameworks also treat E, S, and G as independent reporting dimensions. The 8 Values integrates Impact Value into the compounding system — it is both generated by and a generator of People Value and Market Value. This systemic relationship is invisible in ESG disclosure.

**ESG gap filled by 8 Values:** Impact is treated as operational, not just reportable. Compounding relationships between impact and other value dimensions are modeled. No equivalent of ESG's prescriptive disclosure standards exists in the 8 Values — organizations need both: 8 Values for operational impact measurement, ESG standards for external accountability.

### OKRs (Objectives & Key Results)

OKRs are a goal-alignment and progress-tracking system, originated at Intel and popularized by Google. Organizations set ambitious Objectives (qualitative direction) and measurable Key Results (quantitative proof), cascaded across the organization at company, team, and individual levels. OKRs are used to align execution across large organizations and track progress against strategic intent.

**Alignment:** OKRs are framework-agnostic — they can be written for any dimension of organizational performance. In practice, OKRs are often written for dimensions that correspond to 8 Values: revenue OKRs (Financial Value), customer acquisition OKRs (Customer Value), hiring and capability OKRs (People Value), product launch OKRs (Innovation Value). OKRs could function as the tracking layer within the 8 Values measurement system — setting objectives that correspond to specific value dimensions and measuring progress through key results that mirror AER and 3E signals.

**Key difference:** OKRs define what the organization is trying to achieve and whether it is making progress. They do not define what organizational value means, how it is generated, or how value dimensions compound. An OKR system tells a team whether they hit their target; the 8 Values tells the organization whether it is generating value and through which mechanisms. OKRs are a goal-setting and execution-tracking system; the 8 Values is a value generation measurement model. The two are complementary: OKRs can be structured to operationalize 8 Values priorities; the 8 Values profile surfaces which dimensions need OKR investment.

OKRs are also independent by design — objectives are set and tracked per team or unit, with alignment through cascading rather than through a modeled compounding system. The 8 Values compounding relationships are invisible to OKR tracking: a team can achieve all of its People Value OKRs while Operational Value is degrading upstream because the OKR system does not model the connection.

**OKR gap filled by 8 Values:** No definition of what organizational value means or how it is composed. No compounding model. No connection to delivery architecture. No bridge to governance or external reporting. OKRs track execution progress; the 8 Values measures what execution produces as organizational value.

## The Distinctive Contribution of the 8 Values

### Operational Grounding

Every framework in this comparison measures outcomes or conditions — they observe what the organization has produced or what state it is in. The 8 Values measures value generated *through specific operational mechanisms*: OSM generates Strategic Value and sets Innovation Value direction; PTS generates People Value, Operational Value, and the Innovation pipeline; CVDM generates Customer Value and Market Value through delivery. Each value dimension can be traced to an operational source. An organization using the 8 Values can answer not just "how much Customer Value are we generating?" but "which value stage in which value stream is failing to deliver customer outcomes, and what does the 3E profile of that stage show?"

This traceability is absent in every other framework. The Balanced Scorecard, EFQM, IIRC, ESG, and OKRs all measure at the outcome layer. The 8 Values measures at the outcome layer and connects backward to the operational source.

### Compounding Model

No established framework models compounding relationships between value dimensions as a structural property of the measurement system. The BSC's strategy maps infer cause-and-effect from strategic logic; the 8 Values specifies directional compounding relationships — People Value health drives Operational Value capability, which drives Customer Value delivery consistency, which drives Financial Value sustainability and Market Value reputation. Impact Value deepens People Value and Market Value simultaneously. Strategic Value direction determines where Innovation Value investment compounds.

This means a gap in any dimension has a diagnosis: trace upstream in the compounding chain to find the root cause before acting on the downstream symptom. No other framework in this comparison provides that diagnostic structure.

### Leading Indicator Architecture

The 8 Values is the third layer of a three-layer measurement sequence: 3E signals surface first (operational delivery), AER signals surface next (behavioral response), 8 Values signals surface last (organizational value generation). This sequence makes the 8 Values a *lagging* measurement of what *leading* signals predicted. An Efficiency gap in a critical capability (3E) predicts an Adoption decline in Organizational AER, which predicts an Operational Value degradation in the 8 Values — before the organizational outcome registers.

Established frameworks are predominantly lagging. The Balanced Scorecard attempts to introduce leading indicators through strategy maps but does not connect them to a delivery architecture that generates behavioral signals in sequence. The 8 Values leading indicator architecture is structural, not inferred.

### Dual Reporting Direction

The 8 Values is the only framework in this comparison that explicitly operates in two reporting directions simultaneously: upstream to internal governance (OSM Governance & Measurement) to validate strategic hypotheses, and outward to external reporting via the IR 6 Capitals bridge. This dual direction means a single measurement model serves both the board's internal accountability function and its external stakeholder reporting function — without requiring separate measurement systems for each.
