# Organization Strategy Model (OSM) — Practitioners Guide

**How to translate strategic intent into prioritized, value-stream-governed outcomes — and govern execution through a continuous strategic feedback loop.**

---

## At a Glance

| | |
|---|---|
| **What it does** | Translates Values, Mission, and Vision into strategic value priorities and the value-stream outcomes the business intends to produce — then governs execution by value stream |
| **When to use it** | Setting or resetting strategy; when execution is drifting from intent; when value priorities are unclear, contested, or not cascading into delivery |
| **Who runs it** | CEO / executive team at the Organization level; Enterprise & Functional leaders and Portfolio leaders at the levels below |
| **Who must be in the room** | Leadership (direction), strategy/transformation lead (facilitation), value-stream owners (executability), risk owner (enterprise risk), finance (resource reality) |
| **Key inputs** | Values, Mission, Vision; internal & external context; stakeholder & customer expectations; leadership direction; enterprise risk; Product Mindset maturity |
| **Key outputs** | Strategic value priorities; value-stream outcome definitions; a Governance & Measurement cadence; the strategic feedback loop |
| **Typical timeframe** | Initial run: a focused strategy cycle (2–6 weeks). Governance cadence: quarterly review, annual reset |

> **Not a product organization?** "Customer" → "citizen / patient / client / beneficiary"; "product team" → "delivery unit"; "value stream" → "care pathway / service delivery stream / value chain." The logic is unchanged — only the vocabulary shifts. See [Translation Notes](#translation-notes).

> **Where this fits:** OSM is the strategy and governance layer of the IIVCS — it **governs** *what* value to create and *where*. PTS **operationalizes** those priorities into an operating model; CVDM **realizes** them as customer outcomes. Outcome definition flows down; outcome evidence flows back up. See [Framework Positioning](../positioning.md).

## Before You Start

**Inputs required**

- [ ] **Values, Mission, and Vision** — the organization's stated identity and direction. If these are absent or stale, OSM surfaces it immediately; resolve before setting priorities.
- [ ] **Internal & external context** — market shifts, competitive position, capability and capacity reality, and Product Mindset maturity (the organization's readiness to operate strategy as a living hypothesis).
- [ ] **Stakeholder & customer expectations** — what customers, the board, regulators, and employees expect the organization to deliver. This is the customer-obsession input to strategy.
- [ ] **Leadership direction** — the executive view on where the organization must place its bets.
- [ ] **Enterprise risk** — the risks (ERM) that constrain or qualify which strategies are realistic.
- [ ] **A current value-stream inventory** — the pre-existing business workflows and capabilities through which value is created. Strategy is governed *through* value streams, so you must know what they are.

**People required**

| Role | Why they must be present |
|---|---|
| CEO / executive sponsor | Owns strategic intent and the final call on value priorities |
| Strategy / transformation lead | Facilitates the Strategic Value Creation process and owns the governance cadence |
| Value-stream owners | Provide the executability reality — whether a priority can actually be carried |
| Enterprise risk owner | Brings the risk constraints that qualify strategic options |
| Finance lead | Grounds priorities in resourcing and investment reality |

**Prepare the room**

- Gather the current Values, Mission, Vision, and last cycle's strategic objectives in advance — OSM refines from a baseline, it does not invent from nothing.
- Assemble the context pack: market/competitive signals, the dual-track AER and 8 Values readings from the prior cycle (if the system is already running), and the enterprise risk register.
- Confirm the mandate: the room must have the authority to set and commit to value priorities. A strategy session without decision authority produces a wish list, not a strategy.

## How to Run It

Each step below is an action. Complete them in order — later steps depend on earlier outputs. OSM runs at three levels (Organization → Enterprise/Function → Portfolio); run the full sequence at the Organization level first, then cascade the same sequence down, with each level aligning to the intent set above it.

---

### Step 1 — Establish strategic foundation and context

**What you do:** Confirm Values, Mission, and Vision, then assemble the five strategic inputs — internal/external context, stakeholder & customer expectations, leadership direction, enterprise risk, and Product Mindset maturity. Reconcile them into a shared, current picture of where the organization stands and what is changing.

**Key question this step answers:** *Who are we, what do we stand for, and what is the context we must create value within right now?*

**Inputs:** Values, Mission, Vision; context pack; stakeholder/customer expectations; risk register
**Output:** Strategic foundation brief — confirmed identity statement plus a synthesized current-context picture

**Guidance:**
- Treat the inputs as live, not static. The most common failure is anchoring on last year's context. Market shifts, stakeholder expectations, and last cycle's delivery outcomes all feed back in here.
- Product Mindset maturity is an internal-context factor, not an aspiration. An organization that cannot yet operate strategy as a testable hypothesis must set priorities it can realistically govern — name the maturity honestly.
- You are done when leadership agrees on a single shared picture of context. If two executives hold materially different pictures of the market or the organization's readiness, resolve it before moving on — divergence here multiplies downstream.

---

### Step 2 — Define strategic value priorities

**What you do:** Run the Strategic Value Creation process: translate the foundation and context into Strategic Objectives expressed as **Outcomes and Value Priorities** — not activities or deliverables. Prioritize them; a strategy that treats everything as a priority governs nothing.

**Key question this step answers:** *Where will we create value, in what order, and what outcome defines success?*

**Inputs:** Strategic foundation brief (Step 1)
**Output:** A ranked set of strategic value priorities, each stated as an outcome

**Guidance:**
- State every priority as an outcome, not an output. "Become the default onboarding choice for mid-market operations teams" is a value priority. "Ship the new onboarding flow" is a deliverable — it belongs downstream in PTS/CVDM.
- Let values act as a constraint. A strategic option that conflicts with the organization's stated Values should be rejected here, not rationalized — this is where customer obsession and integrity enter strategy.
- Rank ruthlessly. The output of this step is an *ordered* list. If leadership will not rank, the prioritization has not happened — it has been deferred to whoever controls resources by default.
- You are done when each priority has a named outcome that someone in the room can say "we would know this succeeded if…" about.

---

### Step 3 — Map priorities to value streams and define value-stream outcomes

**What you do:** Assign each strategic value priority to the value stream(s) that will carry it. For each value stream, define the **value-stream outcome** — the business intent that value stream now exists to produce. Then cascade: at the Enterprise/Function and Portfolio levels, align local priorities and value-stream outcomes to the intent set above.

**Key question this step answers:** *Through which value streams will each priority be created, and what must each value stream produce?*

**Inputs:** Ranked value priorities (Step 2); value-stream inventory
**Output:** Priority-to-value-stream map; a defined value-stream outcome per affected value stream; cascaded alignment across levels

**Guidance:**
- The value-stream outcome is set by the business, not constituted by the sum of downstream delivery. OSM *defines* it; PTS owns its delivery; customer outcomes *evidence* it. Keep that distinction — it prevents strategy from being quietly redefined by whatever teams happen to deliver.
- Every priority must land on at least one value stream with a named owner. A priority that maps to no value stream is an intention with no path to execution — either find the value stream or drop the priority.
- When cascading, each lower level aligns *to* the level above — it does not re-litigate it. The Portfolio level decides how to allocate across value streams to serve Enterprise/Function intent; it does not reopen organization-level priorities.
- You are done when every committed priority traces to a value stream, every affected value stream has a stated outcome, and the cascade is consistent top to bottom.

---

### Step 4 — Establish Governance & Measurement and hand off to execution

**What you do:** Hand the value-stream outcomes to execution (PTS builds the operating model to deliver them). Set up Governance & Measurement: define how 3E signal, the dual-track AER (Organizational AER from PTS, Customer AER from CVDM), and the 8 Values will be read at the strategic level to tell you whether priorities are generating their intended outcomes.

**Key question this step answers:** *How will we know whether each strategic priority is actually generating the value we intended?*

**Inputs:** Value-stream outcome definitions (Step 3)
**Output:** Governance & Measurement setup — the strategic-level measurement frame, the reconciliation model, and the handoff to PTS

**Guidance:**
- Measure outcomes, not activity. Governance & Measurement reads whether value-stream outcomes are being produced — not whether teams are busy. Effectiveness (outcome achieved) is OSM's proof; Experience (is the strategy coherent and believed?) is its leading early-warning.
- Do not build a parallel behavioral track. OSM does not generate its own AER; it is the reconciliation point where PTS's Organizational AER and CVDM's Customer AER converge, expressed through the 8 Values. Define *where they converge* and *who reads them together*.
- Make the handoff explicit. PTS must receive each value-stream outcome with its owner and its success definition. An outcome handed off without a measurement frame returns no usable signal.
- You are done when each value-stream outcome has: a strategic measurement frame, a named convergence point for Org AER + Customer AER + 8 Values, and a confirmed PTS owner.

---

### Step 5 — Activate the strategic feedback loop

**What you do:** Define the cadence, owners, and triggers for the continuous loop between Governance & Measurement and Execution & Deployment. This is where PTS's executability signal, CVDM's customer reality, the dual-track AER, and the 8 Values converge — and resolve into confirmation, refutation, or retuning of strategic hypotheses.

**Key question this step answers:** *How and when will strategy be reassessed as execution generates signal?*

**Inputs:** Governance & Measurement setup (Step 4); the return path from PTS and CVDM
**Output:** Strategic governance cadence — review frequency, named owners, out-of-cycle triggers, and the reassessment mechanism

**Guidance:**
- Strategy is a living hypothesis. The loop's job is to test whether each strategic bet is generating the expected outcome — and to retune when it is not. A governance cadence that only reports status, never revises priorities, is not OSM.
- Set at minimum two out-of-cycle triggers: an **executability signal** from PTS (a value-stream outcome the operating model cannot carry) and a **customer-reality signal** from CVDM (strategic-evolution feedback that contradicts a priority's premise). Either should trigger an out-of-cycle strategic review.
- Name who acts, not just who receives. The CEO/executive team owns acting on the reconciled signal. If reconciliation lands in a report no one is accountable for acting on, the loop is decorative.
- Minimum cadence: quarterly governance review, annual strategic reset — shortened whenever an out-of-cycle trigger fires.

## Worked Examples

---

### Example 1 — B2B SaaS scaleup resetting strategy (Organization + Portfolio levels)

> **Scenario:** A Series C workflow-automation SaaS company has grown to 400 people. The strategy lives in a board deck, but engineering, sales, and customer success are each optimizing for different goals. Growth is slowing and no one can say which bets are working. The CEO runs OSM to reset and govern strategy.

**Step 1 — Establish foundation and context**
Values, Mission, Vision confirmed (Mission: "make operations teams self-sufficient"). Context synthesis: the mid-market segment is saturating; enterprise deals are growing but onboarding is failing them; Product Mindset maturity is moderate — teams run sprints but rarely test strategic assumptions. Enterprise risk: over-reliance on two large logos (38% of ARR).

**Step 2 — Define strategic value priorities**
Ranked outcomes:
1. *Become the default automation platform for enterprise operations teams* (growth).
2. *Reduce revenue concentration below 25% from top two accounts* (resilience).
3. *Make onboarding self-serve for enterprise, not just mid-market* (the enabler of #1).
A fourth proposed priority — "launch an analytics add-on" — was rejected: it was a deliverable, not an outcome, and conflicted with focus.

**Step 3 — Map to value streams and define outcomes**
- Priority #1 + #3 → **Customer Onboarding value stream**. Value-stream outcome: *Enterprise customers reach first automated workflow without professional-services involvement within 14 days.*
- Priority #2 → **Expansion & Retention value stream**. Value-stream outcome: *Net revenue retention ≥ 120% across the mid-market base, broadening the revenue base.*
- Cascade: the Portfolio level allocated two product teams to Onboarding and one to Expansion, aligning to the organization-level ranking.

**Step 4 — Governance & Measurement and handoff**
Each value-stream outcome handed to PTS with an owner. Strategic measurement frame: Effectiveness read as the value-stream outcome (14-day enterprise activation; NRR). Convergence point defined: a monthly strategic review reads Organizational AER (are teams adopting the new value-stream operating model?) alongside Customer AER (are enterprise customers adopting, engaging, retaining?), expressed through Strategic and Customer Value.

**Step 5 — Activate the feedback loop**
- Quarterly governance review; annual reset.
- Out-of-cycle trigger #1 (executability): if PTS reports the Onboarding value stream cannot hit 14-day activation with current capabilities → strategic review of priority #3's resourcing.
- Out-of-cycle trigger #2 (customer reality): if CVDM strategic-evolution feedback shows enterprise customers fundamentally need PS involvement → revisit the self-serve premise.
- Owner: CEO acts on reconciled signal; strategy lead owns the cadence.

> **What it produced:** Three ranked value priorities, each owned by a named value stream with a measurable outcome; a monthly dual-track reconciliation; and — at the first quarterly review — an evidence-based decision to slow priority #2 and reinforce #3 when Org AER showed teams had not adopted the new onboarding operating model.

### Example 2 — National public-service agency modernization (Organization + Enterprise/Function levels, Government)

> **Scenario:** A national agency delivering citizen benefits is under pressure to modernize. Digital uptake is low, call centers are overwhelmed, and three directorates are each running disconnected transformation programs. The Director-General runs OSM to set coherent strategic priorities and govern them across the agency.

**Step 1 — Establish foundation and context** *(Translation: "customer" → "citizen"; "value stream" → "service delivery stream.")*
Mission confirmed: "deliver every entitled benefit accurately, on time, and with dignity." Context: 70% of citizens still transact by phone or in person; legacy systems block integration; risk register flags a major audit exposure on payment accuracy. Product Mindset maturity: low — the agency runs projects, not continuous discovery.

**Step 2 — Define strategic value priorities**
Ranked outcomes:
1. *Citizens can complete the most common benefit applications end-to-end without phoning or visiting an office.*
2. *Payment accuracy reaches and sustains 99.5%, closing the audit exposure.*
3. *The three directorates govern transformation as one coherent portfolio, not three.*

**Step 3 — Map to service delivery streams and define outcomes**
- Priority #1 → **Benefit Application service delivery stream**. Outcome: *80% of high-volume applications completed through digital self-service to successful determination.*
- Priority #2 → **Payment & Eligibility service delivery stream**. Outcome: *Sustained 99.5% payment accuracy with traceable eligibility decisions.*
- Priority #3 → governed via the Enterprise/Function cascade: each directorate aligns its program to the two outcomes above rather than its own roadmap.

**Step 4 — Governance & Measurement and handoff**
Outcomes handed to the agency's delivery model (PTS equivalent). Strategic frame: Effectiveness read as the service-stream outcomes (digital completion rate; payment accuracy). Convergence point: a cross-directorate governance board reads Organizational AER (are delivery teams adopting the shared operating model?) and Citizen AER (are citizens adopting, engaging with, and continuing to use digital channels?), expressed through Customer (citizen), Operational, and Impact Value.

**Step 5 — Activate the feedback loop**
- Quarterly governance board; annual strategic reset aligned to the budget cycle.
- Out-of-cycle trigger #1: a directorate's program diverges from the two agency outcomes → board realignment.
- Out-of-cycle trigger #2: citizen digital-channel retention falls (citizens revert to phone) → strategic review of the self-service premise and accessibility.
- Owner: Director-General acts on reconciled signal; the transformation office owns the cadence.

> **What it produced:** Two citizen-facing service-stream outcomes plus a portfolio-coherence priority that ended the three-directorate divergence; a dual-track (organizational + citizen) reconciliation board; and a governance mechanism that reads accuracy and dignity as outcomes, not project milestones.

## Measurement

### 3E Application

OSM is measured at the strategic level. **Effectiveness is OSM's proof** — whether strategic priorities generate their intended value-stream outcomes. **Experience is its leading early-warning** — whether leaders, teams, and stakeholders find the strategy coherent and credible enough to execute. **Efficiency is the table stake** — whether the strategy and governance process itself runs without waste or conflicting direction.

| 3E Dimension | What It Measures Here | Primary Signal |
|---|---|---|
| **Efficiency — Use** | Does the strategy process and governance run without friction — priorities set, cascaded, and governed without delay, duplication, or conflicting direction? | Leading |
| **Experience — Feel** | Do leaders, teams, and stakeholders experience the strategy as clear, coherent, and credible — do they understand and believe the value priorities? | Leading |
| **Effectiveness — Achieve** | Are the strategic value priorities producing their intended value-stream outcomes and organizational value? | Lagging |

**Primary 3E signal for this framework:** Effectiveness — OSM exists to make strategic bets that achieve outcomes. But because Effectiveness is slow to confirm at the strategic level, watch Experience as the leading signal: a strategy teams do not understand or believe will fail in execution long before the outcome data confirms it.

### 8 Values Generated

This framework most directly drives:

| Value | How This Framework Generates It |
|---|---|
| **Strategic Value** | OSM is the source of Strategic Value — it sets, governs, and validates the strategic hypotheses the whole system serves |
| **Innovation Value** | OSM sets the direction for Innovation Value — value priorities determine where the organization places its innovation bets, even though PTS most directly generates it |

All 8 Values are touched across the full IIVCS — and OSM is uniquely the **reconciliation point** where every value dimension converges to validate strategy. The table above shows which Values OSM is the primary generator and direction-setter of.

### AER Connection

OSM does not generate a behavioral track of its own. It is the reconciliation point where **Organizational AER** (from PTS — whether teams adopt, engage with, and sustain the operating model) and **Customer AER** (from CVDM — whether customers adopt, engage with, and retain the value) converge in Governance & Measurement, expressed through the 8 Values. When *both* tracks reach sustained Retention on a value stream, the strategic priority behind it is validated — the strategy produced organizational value that lasted. When the tracks diverge — teams adopt an operating model that yields no customer retention, or customers retain on delivery the operating model cannot sustain — OSM reads the divergence as a refuted or mis-resourced strategic hypothesis and retunes.

## System Health Check

These signals confirm OSM is working — not just being followed. Review them at each governance cadence.

| Signal | Healthy | At Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Priorities stated as outcomes | Every value priority is an outcome someone can define success for | Priorities are activities, deliverables, or initiatives ("launch X") |
| Priority-to-value-stream traceability | Every committed priority maps to a value stream with a named owner | Priorities exist with no value stream or owner to carry them |
| Cascade coherence | Enterprise/Function and Portfolio levels align to the level above | Lower levels run their own priorities disconnected from organization intent |
| Loop revises, not just reports | Governance reviews change priorities and resourcing on signal | Reviews report status; priorities never change regardless of what signal shows |
| Dual-track reconciliation | Org AER and Customer AER are read together against each priority | Only one track (or only activity/output) is reviewed |

**If two or more signals are At Risk:** Stop and return to Step 2. The strategy is not governing execution — it is a list of intentions running in parallel to delivery. Re-establish outcome-stated priorities, value-stream traceability, and a loop with the authority to revise before the next governance cycle.

## Translation Notes

### Non-product contexts

OSM applies to any organization that must turn intent into governed value creation. The following table maps default IIVCS language to equivalent terms by sector:

| IIVCS Default | Healthcare | Government | Professional Services | Manufacturing | Financial Services |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Customer | Patient / member | Citizen / resident | Client | End customer / buyer | Client / policyholder |
| Value priority | Care outcome priority | Public-service outcome | Client-impact priority | Product/market priority | Portfolio/segment priority |
| Value stream | Care pathway | Service delivery stream | Client delivery lifecycle | Value chain / production line | Service fulfillment stream |
| Value-stream outcome | Population health outcome | Service outcome standard | Engagement outcome | Throughput / quality outcome | Servicing / growth outcome |
| Governance & Measurement | Clinical governance | Performance & assurance board | Engagement governance | Operations governance | Risk & performance governance |

> **Core principle:** The logic is unchanged across sectors — translate intent into prioritized, outcome-stated value, govern it through value streams, and revise it on dual-track signal. Only the vocabulary for "customer," "value stream," and "outcome" shifts.

## Quick Reference

**Steps**

1. Establish strategic foundation and context — *Who are we and what context must we create value in?*
2. Define strategic value priorities — *Where will we create value, and what outcome defines success?*
3. Map priorities to value streams and define outcomes — *Through which value streams, and what must each produce?*
4. Establish Governance & Measurement and hand off — *How will we know each priority is generating value?*
5. Activate the strategic feedback loop — *How and when will strategy be reassessed on signal?*

**Health signals at a glance**

- Every priority is stated as an outcome, not an activity
- Every priority traces to a value stream with a named owner
- Lower levels align to the level above — the cascade is coherent
- The governance loop revises priorities, not just reports status
- Organizational AER and Customer AER are reconciled together against each priority

**Primary 3E signal:** Effectiveness — OSM is proven by strategic outcomes achieved; watch Experience (strategy coherence and belief) as the leading early-warning.

**Primary 8 Values driven:** Strategic Value (source), Innovation Value (direction-setter)

**Where OSM sits:** OSM governs *what* value to create and *where*; PTS operationalizes it; CVDM realizes it. Feedback runs upstream — CVDM → PTS → OSM — and resolves into strategic reassessment here. See [Framework Positioning](../positioning.md).
